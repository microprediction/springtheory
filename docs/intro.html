The Analog Kalman Filter

From Hooke's law to Bayes' rule, one spring at a time.

1. A Hookean universe

Work in one dimension and suppose a slightly unusual physics. A body of mass $k$ at position $x$ pulls a unit test particle at $z$ with a force that grows linearly with the separation:

$$ F(z) \;=\; k\,(x - z), \qquad U(z) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,k\,(z - x)^2 . $$

Call $k$ a mass in linear gravity, or the stiffness of a spring tying the particle to an anchor at $x$. The algebra is Hooke's law either way. Hooke published that law in 1678 as ut tensio, sic vis. As the extension, so the force. He had kept it hidden for two years as a scrambled anagram, so that no one could claim it ahead of him. Now set the potential $U$ next to a Gaussian density $\mathcal{N}(x, k^{-1})$ read off at $z$:

$$ -\log p(z) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,k\,(z - x)^2 + \text{const}. $$

They agree up to a constant. So in this universe, energy is a negative log-likelihood, mass is precision, and force is the score $\tfrac{d}{dz}\log p(z)$. Minimising energy maximises likelihood. A particle let go to find its rest point is doing maximum a posteriori estimation by analog computation. Gauss reached the same quadratic from the far side in 1809. He asked which error law makes the arithmetic mean the most probable estimate of a quantity. The normal distribution is the answer. That is roughly why least squares and Gaussian errors have travelled together ever since. Legendre had printed the method in 1805. He was not pleased when Gauss claimed to have been using it privately since 1795. The dictionary lists the rest of the correspondences.

2. Fusion: the centre of mass is Bayes' rule

Tie the test particle to two bodies. A prior of mass $P^{-1}$ anchored at $m$, and an observation of mass $R^{-1}$ anchored at $y$. The net force is zero where the two pulls balance:

$$ P^{-1}(m - z) + R^{-1}(y - z) = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; z^\ast \;=\; \frac{P^{-1}m + R^{-1}y}{P^{-1} + R^{-1}} . $$

That is the centre of mass of the two bodies. It is also the Kalman measurement update. In gain form, with $K = P/(P+R)$, the same point reads $z^\ast = m + K(y - m)$. The posterior precision is the total mass, $(P')^{-1} = P^{-1} + R^{-1}$. Masses hung in parallel add. That is what independent precisions adding looks like once you build it out of springs.

This buys the first consolidation move. As far as the test particle can tell, the two bodies are now one body of mass $P^{-1}+R^{-1}$ sitting at $z^\ast$. No measurement it can make will pull them apart again. Consolidation is the part that earns its keep. Without it you would have a nice picture and no algorithm. It is what “the posterior is again Gaussian” looks like in brass. Watch it settle in the demo.

3. Chains: series springs are the reduced mass

Make the model hierarchical, as in the article. The location $\mu$ has a Gaussian prior. An intermediate quantity $x_2$ scatters around $\mu$. The measurement $y$ is a noisy reading of $x_2$:

$$ \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(m, P), \qquad x_2 \mid \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \rho^{-1}), \qquad y \mid x_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(x_2, \phi^{-1}). $$

Now the body at $y$ does not touch the test particle. Its spring of stiffness $\phi$ ends at a free joint, and a second spring of stiffness $\rho$ carries on to the particle. Two springs in series have effective stiffness

$$ \tilde{\phi} \;=\; \left( \frac{1}{\phi} + \frac{1}{\rho} \right)^{-1} \;=\; \frac{\phi\,\rho}{\phi + \rho}, $$

the reduced mass $\mu_{12} = m_1 m_2 / (m_1 + m_2)$ from the two-body problem. It is the trick for turning two orbiting masses into one fictitious body about a fixed point. Here it says only that variances add along a chain. Marginally, $y \mid \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu,\, \phi^{-1} + \rho^{-1})$. Galton built the mechanical version in the 1870s. His two-stage quincunx dropped lead shot through one bank of pins and then a second. It showed that a normal spread laid on top of a normal spread is still normal, the whole compounding argument done in wood. The second consolidation move follows. Replace the chain by a single body of mass $\tilde{\phi}$ at $y$, then apply the centre-of-mass rule from step 2. In the demo the chain and its consolidation settle on the same line.

$$ m' \;=\; \frac{P^{-1}m + \tilde{\phi}\,y}{P^{-1} + \tilde{\phi}}, \qquad (P')^{-1} \;=\; P^{-1} + \tilde{\phi}. $$

4. The Kalman filter as a limit

Stiffen the middle link. As $\rho \to \infty$ the joint locks and $x_2$ sits on $\mu$. The reduced mass returns $\tilde{\phi} \to \phi$, the plain update of step 2. The textbook Kalman step is the rigid-rod limit of a hierarchical model. Keep $\rho$ finite and you keep the hierarchy, at the price of one harmonic mean. The fusion demo has a $\rho$ dial. Slacken the channel and watch the observation's effective mass drain away.

5. The whole filter: parallel and series in turn

One ingredient is still missing, which is time. Between observations the state diffuses with process noise of variance $Q$, so the predictive variance is $P' + Q$. In mass terms that is a second series composition, now against the process-noise body of mass $q = Q^{-1}$:

$$ p_{\text{pred}} \;=\; \frac{p'\,q}{p' + q} \;<\; p'. $$

The scalar Kalman filter is then the two rules taking turns. Measurement updates add mass in parallel, and evidence builds. Time updates bleed mass through a series spring, and the estimate goes stale. The state is a body that gains weight at every observation and loses it at every tick of the clock. Kalman published this in 1960. He was not quite first. T. N. Thiele had the same recursion in 1880, in a paper on reducing astronomical observations. Norbert Wiener had solved the stationary version during the war, working on how to aim anti-aircraft guns. The older habit is the mechanical one. Long before the digital machine, people solved a problem by building a system that relaxed into the answer, then read off where it stopped.

6. Beyond one dimension

None of this was really scalar. In $n$ dimensions mass becomes a precision matrix, springs pull along vectors, and the two rules become $\Sigma$-weighted averages and matrix harmonic means. Condition a multivariate Gaussian on some of its coordinates. Mechanically, you pin part of the spring network to the wall and ask where the rest settles. Out comes a Schur complement of the precision matrix. Schur wrote down the determinant identity behind it in 1917. The name Schur complement is much younger. Emilie Haynsworth coined it in 1968. Try it on a small graph. That thread also runs at the sister site, schur.microprediction.org.

Caveats

The correspondence holds when the energies are quadratic. That means everything is Gaussian and linear, and not otherwise. A non-Gaussian likelihood is a non-Hookean spring. Forces still balance at a mode, so maximum a posteriori estimation as equilibrium survives. The consolidation moves do not survive. You can no longer swap a subsystem for one equivalent body. The analogy fails where the mathematics fails, at non-conjugacy. That is worth knowing as much as anything it gets right.
