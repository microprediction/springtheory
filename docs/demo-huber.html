Huber loss

A spring that gives up trying, past a threshold.

Five observations, one shared parameter $z$, three rails. Each observation pulls on $z$ with the same spring shape on a given rail, and the three rails differ only in that shape:

$$ F_{\text{L2}}(y,z) = \phi(y-z), \qquad F_{\text{L1}}(y,z) = \phi\,\mathrm{sign}(y-z), \qquad F_{\text{Huber}}(y,z) = \begin{cases} \phi(y-z) & |y-z|\le\delta \\ \phi\,\delta\,\mathrm{sign}(y-z) & |y-z|>\delta \end{cases} $$

L2 (top) is an ordinary Hookean spring per point: the equilibrium is the plain mean, and one wild point drags it arbitrarily far. L1 (bottom) is five constant-tension pulleys, exactly like the Lasso demo's device: the equilibrium is the median, provably immovable by any single point no matter how extreme. Huber (middle) is the practical compromise: an ordinary spring for small residuals, but past a threshold $\delta$ it switches to the same constant pull as L1 — the spring notices it's dealing with an outlier and stops trying so hard.

Drag the rightmost point far away and watch: the L2 hub follows it linearly, without limit; the Huber hub moves, then flattens out once the residual passes $\delta$; the L1 hub barely notices at all. Slide $\delta$ to $0$ and the Huber rail becomes L1; slide it past the data's spread and it becomes L2 — one threshold, the same two extremes as the exponent dial reaches by reshaping the whole curve instead of capping it.

L2 (mean) Huber L1 (median)

Huber (1964) introduced exactly this loss to get a robust location estimator with a bounded influence function but without discarding the efficiency of the mean when data really are clean — the compromise this page makes mechanical.
