Least angle regression

Lower the tension slowly, and predictors engage one at a time — each held at exactly the ceiling.

The Lasso demo replaced the shrinkage spring with a constant-tension pulley of force $\lambda$: a predictor sits at exactly zero until its correlation with the residual exceeds $\lambda$, at which point it engages. Least angle regression (LARS) is what you get when you take that one threshold and lower it continuously from very high to zero, watching the whole configuration re-form as it descends.

The Karush–Kuhn–Tucker conditions for the Lasso say exactly this: at penalty $\lambda$, every active predictor has $|x_j^{\top} r| = \lambda$ — its correlation with the residual is pinned to the ceiling — while every idle one sits strictly below, $|x_j^{\top} r| \le \lambda$. So as $\lambda$ falls the active predictors march down together, always tied, always equal to the current tension:

$$ x_j^{\top}\!\left(y - X\beta\right) = \lambda\,\operatorname{sign}(\beta_j)\ \ (j\ \text{active}), \qquad \left|x_k^{\top}\!\left(y - X\beta\right)\right| \le \lambda\ \ (k\ \text{idle}). $$

The name is the mechanism. To keep every active correlation equal as the fit advances, the fitted vector must move along the direction that makes equal angles with all active predictors — the least angle, equiangular direction. Move that way and the tied correlations decay in lockstep; an idle predictor stays out until the descending ceiling reaches its correlation, and then it joins. Between two such joins the coefficients are exactly linear in $\lambda$, so the whole solution path is piecewise-linear with a kink at each engagement — and it is the Lasso path, traced from the top down.

Top: the descending ceiling. Each line is one predictor's absolute correlation with the current residual, $|x_j^{\top} r|$, plotted against $\lambda$ (which runs high→low, left to right). The dashed diagonal is the ceiling $|c| = \lambda$ itself. Idle predictors ride below it; the moment a line touches the ceiling that predictor engages and then travels down the diagonal, glued to $\lambda$ — tied with every other active one. Bottom: the coefficient path, sharing the same $\lambda$ axis, so the dotted sweep line links the two — each $\beta_j$ leaves zero at exactly the $\lambda$ where, directly above, its correlation hit the ceiling, and every segment is a straight line. Drag $\lambda$, or press Lower the ceiling, and watch the four predictors engage one after another; at $\lambda = 0$ the path arrives at the ordinary least-squares fit.

Efron, Hastie, Johnstone & Tibshirani (2004) introduced LARS precisely as this descent: a single, exact, order-$p$ sweep that produces the entire Lasso regularisation path, one equiangular segment per active-set change — the constant-tension device of the previous demo, filmed as its weight is slowly lifted.
